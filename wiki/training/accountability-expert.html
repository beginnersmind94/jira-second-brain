Accountability for new PMs

A primer for a product manager who has never worked in K-12 school nutrition. You know what a JIRA ticket, a roadmap, and a pricing page are — you don't yet know why cafeteria software is a real industry with real money and real stakes. This guide fixes that in ~45 minutes.

Read in order. Click interactives. Come back tomorrow for the flashcards (chapter 10) — spacing is how ideas actually stick.

Source material for the industry numbers in this guide: USDA Economic Research Service, The National School Lunch Program: Background, Trends, and Issues, 2024 Edition (Report EIB-279, Sept 2024) by Toossi, Todd, Guthrie, & Ollinger — plus patterns read directly from our own 639 Accountability tickets. When a number looks surprising, cross-check EIB-279; it's the authoritative recent data.
Chapter 1

The business model (this is the whole game)

TL;DR The USDA pays schools real money — a few dollars per meal served — when the meal meets federal rules and the kid is eligible. School districts run on thin margins; this reimbursement is a huge share of their nutrition budget. Our product, Accountability, is how districts earn that money by counting, proving, and claiming it.

How big is this market, really?

The National School Lunch Program (NSLP) is the second-largest federal food assistance program in the U.S., after SNAP. From USDA ERS report EIB-279:

MetricValue (FY 2019, pre-COVID)
Schools participating~100,000 (73% of all U.S. schools)
Students served per day29.6 million
Lunches per year4.9 billion
Federal spending$14.4 billion ($2.96/lunch average)
Share of lunches free/reduced74.1%

By FY 2022 (post-pandemic), federal spending had climbed to $21.4 billion. Every one of those dollars flowed through software like ours.

The real 2023-24 reimbursement rates

These are the exact rates districts get per lunch (contiguous U.S., Table 2 of EIB-279):

Meal typeBase rate"Performance-based" (certified to meet nutrition standards)Low-free-share (≥60% free/reduced) + performance
Free lunch$4.25$4.33$4.35
Reduced-price lunch$3.85$3.93$3.95

Alaska rates are roughly 60% higher; Hawaii/Guam/PR/USVI ~30% higher. Rates adjust every July for inflation. Our calculator in Chapter 4 uses these real rates.

Who's actually paying our customer? (revenue mix)

For a typical School Food Authority (SFA) in the 2014-15 school year (most recent USDA national data):

Revenue sourceShare of SFA revenue
Federal reimbursements (NSLP + SBP)56.7%
Student payments (paid + à la carte)20.0%
Competitive food sales (vending, school stores)10.9%
USDA Foods in Schools (in-kind commodities)5.9%

PM takeaway: over half our customer's revenue depends on the federal reimbursement stream we manage. That's why our bugs get CEO attention. It's also why "nice-to-haves" stay in backlog forever — the moment a claim is at risk, roadmap narrows to one item.

And what does it cost SFAs to produce a meal?

  • Labor: 54% of total cost — the biggest line, often unionized, inflexible
  • Food: 29.3% — hit hardest by 2022-23 inflation
  • Other direct (non-food supplies, equipment): 7.8%
  • Indirect (facilities, district admin): 8.9%

Because labor is so dominant and so inflexible, SFAs can't easily cut costs. They live or die by volume × reimbursement rate. That's the entire financial picture.

The squeeze our customers are living through right now: in the 2016-17 school year, 71% of SFAs broke even or had a surplus. By Q1 of 2022-23 (post-pandemic, high inflation), only 62% did. Small SFAs fared worst. When you hear "we can't afford a price increase" on a renewal call, this is why.

Your customer's profit center, in dollars

A district with 2,500 daily lunches (using 2023-24 base rates):

  • 1,200 Free × $4.25 = $5,100
  • 400 Reduced × $3.85 = $1,540
  • 900 Paid × $0.40 = $360
  • = $7,000/day just from NSLP → roughly $1.26M/year. Add breakfast (SBP) and you're at $1.9M+.
One-sentence frame: we sell software that turns cafeteria activity into federal revenue — with an audit trail the USDA will accept.

The regulatory tsunami (2010 → now)

Three policy events shape every Accountability feature you'll touch:

  1. HHFKA 2010 (Healthy, Hunger-Free Kids Act) — updated nutrition standards, created the Community Eligibility Provision (CEP) enabling universal free meals for high-poverty schools, expanded direct certification via SNAP/TANF/Medicaid, mandated 3-year SFA audits (now 5-year as of July 2024).
  2. COVID waivers (2020-22) — free meals for all students regardless of eligibility; expired June 2022 and hit SFAs like a cliff.
  3. 2024 Nutrition Rule — tightened sodium limits, added-sugar standards, revised whole-grain rules. New reports, new validations, new edge cases.

Any time Sam the State Coordinator asks for a new report, assume it's one of these three ancestors surfacing in a new skin.

How USDA actually watches us (APEC)

USDA runs the Access, Participation, Eligibility, and Certification (APEC) study periodically to measure improper payments — over- and under-reimbursements. The last published study (APEC-III, 2017-18 data):

  • Gross improper payments: 8.02% ($1.09B of total federal reimbursements)
  • Certification errors alone: 6.52% (the biggest category)
  • Aggregation errors: 1.5%
  • Meal claiming errors: 7.6% (doesn't affect money — but flags "standard of service" failures)

Any Accountability feature that reduces certification or aggregation error directly improves customer reimbursement accuracy. That's not polish — that's revenue for the customer and a retention lever for us.

The competitive set

Names you'll hear in customer calls:

  • PrimeroEdge (classic) — our own legacy product; Perseus/NXT is the rewrite
  • Titan School Solutions (LINQ) — largest independent alternative
  • Heartland School Solutions (MCS, Horizon)
  • Nutrikids / Harris School Nutrition
  • MealTime / Cybersoft

Switching is painful — districts keep vendors for 10+ years because migrating audit history is terrifying. Inertia looks like loyalty; NPS is a lagging signal. Drive satisfaction early or pay for it through CS forever.

The 3-second transaction (customer journey walkthrough)

TL;DR Play with a live POS. Pick a student, build a tray, see what the system enforces. Every rule you see maps to a competing business concern — your job as PM is to know which concern wins when they conflict.

Before you ship anything in Accountability, run the simulator at least once. If a UI change slows a cashier by 0.5 seconds, multiplied across a 2,500-meal district, that's ~20 minutes of cafeteria line time per day. Cashiers notice. Site managers notice. Dana notices.

PM observations, in order:
  1. Eligibility drives the meal's unit economics. Same tray, wildly different revenue per customer.
  2. "Free-with-meal" sides are a USDA meal pattern rule — your eng team didn't make that up; it's federal law.
  3. Charging a Paid student's account to negative is a policy switch. Some districts allow it; some don't. That's configurability, which means a settings screen, which means test coverage, which means you care.
  4. À la carte lives in its own revenue bucket. Don't let anyone fold it into "meal revenue".

PM judgment call

Sales asks: "Big district wants us to let cashiers override meal-pattern rules and still mark the meal reimbursable — their union contract is blocking compliance training." How do you answer?
Some features exist to protect the customer from themselves. Allowing this would expose the district to federal fraud liability and potentially us too. New PMs sometimes try to please Sales by finding clever workarounds — here, the correct PM move is a principled no with a concrete alternative path.

PM sanity math: a 1% data-quality error (misclassified meals, uncounted transactions, rejected claims) on a $150K/month claim is $1,500/month / $18K/year of real lost revenue per customer. Multiply by customer count. Data-quality features aren't polish — they're revenue protection.

Edit Check Worksheet = sanity check before you cash the check

Districts are federally required to cross-check their own counts before submitting a claim. The Edit Check Worksheet compares claimed meals vs. theoretically possible meals (Eligible students × attendance × serving days). If claimed exceeds plausible, USDA will kick it back or audit.

This is why ticket NXT-70587 added Total Enrollment count to the worksheet — directors want the plausibility anchor visible before submit. Tiny UX tweak, outsized business value.

How to read an Accountability ticket like a PM

TL;DR You'll read thousands of tickets. Most are noise — bugs, small friction fixes, compliance tweaks. A handful reveal product direction. Here's a quick taxonomy so you don't spend your first month drowning.
If the title starts with...It's probably...PM action
FO -Financial Oversight workstreamTrack for migration from classic PE
Acc - Reports -Report tweakSkim AC; if ≤1 AC bullet, delegate
POS -Cashier-facing changeRead the AC carefully — frontline UX
Acc > End of DayClosing-period workflowCompliance-adjacent; don't ship half
Claims -State/federal reportingOften regulatory — deadline-driven
State abbreviation (Texas CACFP, CA SNP)State-specific complianceNon-negotiable scope, tight timeline
Freshdesk: in bodySupport-originated bugReal customer pain; read the freshdesk ticket linked

Anatomy of a good Accountability ticket

  1. Persona-framed user story — "As Dana the Director I want X so that Y". If Y is weak, push back on scope.
  2. Business rationale — "Without this, we over-claim / miss the deadline / lose an audit." If missing, the ticket is probably an ops-level tweak, not a product priority.
  3. Explicit AC — with checkboxes. Tickets without AC become expensive during dev.
  4. Links to Freshdesk or state guidance — proof the ask is real, not a one-off request.
PM takeaway: when you write a ticket, imagine it'll be read in 2 years by someone trying to understand why the feature exists. The user story is for empathy; the rationale is for archaeology.

You just inherited the backlog

Four tickets, all marked "Medium" priority. Which do you work first?
Regulatory tickets have hard deadlines set by the government, not by you. Miss one, and every Texas district can't claim. The others can wait a sprint or three. Always grep your backlog for state-name + "deadline" before planning.

TL;DR A PM is paid to sort, not to build. Below are five real-ish scenarios hitting your inbox at once. Drag each to the right bucket. The right answers reflect industry-specific priority logic you'll use weekly.

Drag each card into Ship now, Deprioritize, or Clarify first.

A district GM says "your meal counts don't match our POS terminal counts." Last month's claim off by ~$2,400.
Internal engineer: "Can we migrate Revenue Report from Crystal Reports to React? Would take ~3 sprints."
Site manager asks for a "simpler End of Day" — no specifics. First time we've heard the ask from this site.
Security: Square OAuth token renewal is broken in production; 40 districts will lose card processing in 14 days when tokens expire.
🚢 Ship now
🔎 Clarify first
💤 Deprioritize

Why these answers

  • State regulatory change (Ship now): a ticking clock set by the government. Miss it and every Texas district can't submit CACFP. This is a trump card over almost everything.
  • Claims discrepancy (Clarify first): $2,400 sounds small but the pattern matters more than the number. Could be a bug affecting everyone. Your first move is to reproduce or scope, not commit to a fix.
  • Crystal→React migration (Deprioritize): real tech debt, but it's engineering-initiated and has no deadline. Unless reporting is already fragile, this belongs in a dedicated platform quarter, not reactive sprints.
  • "Simpler End of Day" (Clarify first): one vague ask from one site = weakest possible signal. Schedule a 20-min call with that site manager before making any product call.
  • OAuth token lifecycle (Ship now): this one is real — see NXT-70218. Production-breaking with a known countdown.
PM reflex: "Ship now" requires a hard deadline, real customer impact, or cash-flow risk. Everything else either needs more data or waits for a planned work block. Most first-year PMs over-ship because they confuse urgency with importance.
Chapter 7

Square integration: a live build-vs-buy case study

TL;DR Perseus integrated with Square for credit-card processing in 2025. Walk through the PM decisions that shaped it — and the ones we got wrong. This is a real case you can study via the ticket history.

The setup

Districts needed to take credit cards at the POS (parents increasingly refuse cash). Options:

  1. Build our own payment rails — PCI compliance, bank integrations, fraud handling. Huge scope. Not our business.
  2. Integrate Stripe — developer-friendly, but school districts prefer physical terminals with receipts.
  3. Integrate Square — has the physical terminals, has OAuth, already trusted by small businesses. Won.

Decisions the tickets reveal

Decision 1: Where does the Square data live?

Two options: (a) pull everything into Perseus hourly, (b) call Square on demand. We chose a hybrid — pull into Perseus so reports work offline, but always show "syncing…" badges. See NXT-70014 for the filter-behavior work that resulted.

Decision 2: Card vs cash — same column or split?

For years the Revenue Report summed them. Tickets NXT-71622 and NXT-71620 split them out — because site managers reconcile cash drawers and the mixed column broke that mental model. This is a good "don't unify things just because they share a type" lesson.

Decision 3: OAuth token renewal (we were late)

Square tokens expire. We shipped the integration without auto-renewal. Tickets NXT-70218 (auto-renew) and NXT-69853 (proactive reminders) were reactive — written after the first expired tokens hit prod. Lesson: integrations = credentials + expiry + error paths. Plan all three or you'll rediscover them via support tickets.

PM takeaway: when you integrate a third-party, you inherit their lifecycle (expirations, rate limits, API deprecations) and their outages. Your roadmap has to budget for it — roughly 20% of integration scope is "keeping it alive" work.

Design call

Your competitor ships a Stripe integration in 3 weeks. Sales pressures you to catch up. What do you do?
New PMs confuse "competitor shipped X" with "customers want X." Almost always the real ask is capability-shaped, not vendor-shaped. Interview before copying.

Chapter 8

Weird-but-critical edges (staff meals, charity accounts, visitors)

TL;DR Not every transaction is a student buying lunch. Staff meals, charity funds, visitors, catering — each has its own rules and its own revenue treatment. Missing these = garbage claims data = angry directors. Small scope, high stakes.

"Special accounts" are non-student identities the system needs to transact for. They exist because forcing everything into "student" would pollute the claims data.

Staff meals

Staff can buy from the cafeteria. The meal is not reimbursable. Revenue appears in the Revenue Report; the meal does not appear in SNP claims. Districts configure the staff price (often full or discounted).

Visitor / parent meals

Full price, revenue only, never reimbursable. This is where "parent-buys-lunch-with-their-kid" transactions go.

Charity account

When a student can't pay and the district has a charity fund, the system charges the charity account. Kid eats; charity balance decreases. See NXT-70588 for how default naming is seeded per new district so the charity account looks correct out of the box.

Catering / contracted meals

Some districts serve daycares or outside orgs via contract. These are invoiced separately, don't count toward NSLP, and live in a different revenue bucket entirely.

PM takeaway: these features look like edge cases in your roadmap ("just staff meals"), but they're the features that make customers feel the product was designed for their real operations. Ignoring them sends the wrong signal: that we're a generic POS, not a school-nutrition-specific system. Moats live in small, obvious-in-hindsight features.

Tricky one

A principal buys a $5 lunch under their staff ID. What appears in the monthly SNP Claim?
Mixing staff into student meal counts = over-claiming = audit risk. This is the kind of quiet business rule you'll need to remember when a PRD ambiguously says "record all meals".

Chapter 9

Compliance as a moat (why "just edit it" is wrong)

TL;DR Accountability stores immutable audit trails. Closed periods can't be edited; deleted transactions become voids, not deletions. This looks annoying until you realize: immutability is the feature our buyers pay for.

Every Accountability record carries:

  • Original value
  • Timestamped change log
  • Who made each change
  • Reason/note for each edit

Districts are audited every 3 years by USDA Administrative Review. If the system lets users erase history, the district can't pass. If the system preserves it, the district sails through. Switching vendors is a 10-year decision partly because of this — moving audit data between systems is terrifying.

PM takeaway: "Let me just edit this one closed entry" is the #1 request you'll dismiss. The right answer is always an adjustment — a new, labeled entry that corrects without erasing. This is in NXT-70214 verbatim: "When a period is closed it should mean closed. We should not be [allowing exception adjustments]." Auditors love this. Reject politely. Point to the adjustment flow.

Three rules you cannot break

Rule 1
Never enable editing a closed period. Post an adjustment in the current period.
Rule 2
Never offer a "delete transaction" action. Offer a "void" — keeps the row, flips a flag.
Rule 3
Never allow shared logins by policy. Audit logs are meaningless if you can't tell who did what.
Positioning note: these rules are why a district picks us over a generic POS. Lean into them in PMM. "We never lose your audit trail" is a real differentiator against newer, consumer-feeling entrants.
