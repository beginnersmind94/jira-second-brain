# Grounding the Machine: Building a Trustworthy AI Content Engine at Cybersoft

*An HBR-style case study on shipping a customer-facing generative-AI tool when "it reads well" is not good enough.*

---

## Synopsis

Cybersoft's Customer Education team set out to turn raw artifacts they already had — training-session transcripts and a Jira backlog for the SchoolCafé 2.0 product — into polished, customer-ready learning guides, generated by an LLM. The promise was obvious: hours of manual writing collapsed into minutes. The trap was just as obvious in hindsight: a customer-facing document that *reads* authoritative but *cites* the wrong source is worse than no document at all.

This case follows the team from an over-engineered first attempt through a sequence of uncomfortable discoveries — a guide that took fourteen minutes and was three times too long, an automated grader that confidently lied, and a "live demo" that would have silently fabricated content the moment someone uploaded a new topic — to an architecture in which mis-citation became *structurally impossible*. The throughline is a single management question: **when do you trust the model, and when do you build a system that doesn't have to?**

---

## Background: the assignment

The product, "Learning Studio," had a deceptively simple job:

> Upload a training transcript for a SchoolCafé module → generate a learning guide (long-form, micro-guide, or one-page TLDR) where **every factual claim is traced to its source in Jira**, at the correct level of trust.

Two facts shaped everything that followed:

1. **The trust hierarchy was non-negotiable.** Jira tickets carry several fields. *Acceptance Criteria* (AC) is the agreed, shipped behavior — the highest-trust source. *Release Notes* (RN) is the customer-facing voice. *Description* is the lowest-trust field: often a pre-scope problem statement that never matched what shipped. The team's own writing standard had a name for the cardinal sin: **citing Description text as if it were Acceptance Criteria.** A real prior incident (ticket NXT-53316) had shipped exactly that.

2. **The asset was bigger than it looked.** The team's "second brain" wiki — thousands of curated ticket files, SME-reviewed navigation guides, concept and workflow pages — had itself been built by ingesting Jira CSV exports. The raw material and the curated knowledge were the same lineage.

The mandate: a demo good enough to show leadership on Thursday, on a credible path to production.

---

## Decision Point 1 — Seven agents, or one?

**Situation.** The first implementation was a seven-agent pipeline (Mapper → PM → SME → Writer → Reviewer, plus Designer and Eval). It was elaborate, and it didn't work: the agents kept falling back to a local CSV instead of reading live Jira.

**The temptation.** Run the seven-agent system "to validate the POC."

**The decision.** Stop. The seven-agent architecture was *not* the POC — it was complexity that obscured the real bug: a `tools:` allowlist in the agent config was silently excluding the Jira MCP connector. The right shape was a lean single-`query()` Generator (Tasks 1–4 in one context) with a *separate* Evaluator holding refusal authority over publishing.

**Lesson.** *Anthropic's own guidance — "find the simplest thing that works; add agents only when you can't hardcode the path" — applies before you write the prompt, not after.* The team had reached for orchestration where a workflow sufficed.

---

## Decision Point 2 — A fast demo, or a real one?

**Situation.** Leadership wanted a demo that ran end-to-end in under two minutes. Early framing conflated two different ideas: speeding up the *demo*, and *caching* for production.

**The decision.** Separate them cleanly. Build standalone demo files that **pre-cache the Jira reads** (captured once from live Jira) while the **LLM genuinely generates** each guide at showtime. Real output, zero Jira network on stage, no risk of failing live. Production caching was explicitly deferred as a later, separate piece of work.

**Consequence.** The team could iterate on quality without touching the production path — and, crucially, the demo's *grounding* was real, not faked.

---

## Decision Point 3 — Why was a guide 74,000 characters long?

**Situation.** The first real run was alarming. A single long-form guide took **522 seconds and produced 74,055 characters** — roughly 20,000 words. The TLDR "one-pager" ran ~5× its 500-word ceiling. An early 14-minute run looked "hung"; it wasn't — buffered output hid genuinely slow generation.

**Root cause.** Not the model — the *spec*. The templates instructed *exhaustive completeness* ("ALL features... no exceptions"). For a 4,977-ticket module, "document everything" meant a manuscript.

**The decision.** Bound the *selection* (top-N by priority; cover what the trainer taught, not every ticket) and make length ceilings win over completeness.

**Lesson.** *"Comprehensive" was a bug, not a feature.* A 20,000-word "guide" is both slow and unusable; the fix improved quality and speed at once.

---

## Decision Point 4 — The grader that lied (the cardinal sin)

**Situation.** Once an automated eval was run, **all three formats failed** on the same thing: Description (Tier-3) text cited as Acceptance Criteria (Tier-1) — the exact cardinal sin. The team verified it by hand against the source fixture: the quoted "AC" strings lived only in the Description field.

**The diagnosis (a reframe).** This was not a "the model needs to think harder" problem. The tool handed the model cleanly tier-labeled fields; the model picked the better-*reading* Description prose and stamped the trusted "AC" label on it. As the team put it: **poor use of metadata.** The tier was a free-text string the model typed — so nothing stopped the lie.

**The decision.** Make the tier a property of the *source*, never something the model writes. This became the project's defining move (see Decision Point 6).

**Lesson.** *Grounding is a tool/interface design problem, not a prompting problem* — a conclusion four independent Anthropic engineering pieces later confirmed ("poka-yoke your tools": design the mistake out).

---

## Decision Point 5 — Output-bound or thinking-bound?

**Situation.** Bounding output (DP3) made the *derivations* faster but, surprisingly, made the long-form *slower* (582s). Telemetry settled it: the long-form emitted **31,428 output tokens but only ~6,000 were visible text.** The other ~25,000 were invisible reasoning.

**The decision & tension.** Lowering reasoning effort (`effort=medium`) cut the long-form to 412s — but introduced *paraphrasing*, which broke verbatim citations. Speed and grounding were, at that moment, in direct conflict.

**Lesson.** *Measure the right thing.* "It's slow" decomposed into "it thinks a lot, invisibly" — a different fix from "it writes too much."

---

## Decision Point 6 — The breakthrough: make mis-citation impossible

**Situation.** Two problems remained intertwined: the long-form **truncated** (a single-turn, ten-section write hit an output ceiling), and the model still paraphrased citations under load.

**The decision (the architecture, "Cell D").** Re-architect generation so the writer **never types quote text or a tier label at all.** Instead:

1. A **quote registry** is built deterministically from the Jira fields — every quotable span tagged with its ticket and true tier.
2. **Section writers emit only `[CITE:id]` markers** (and are given no tools, so they cannot wander).
3. A **deterministic assembler** renders the exact verbatim quote and correct tier from the registry.
4. Writing is **split section-by-section** (parallel), eliminating the truncation ceiling.

**Result.** Mis-citation became *structurally impossible*: the model has no channel to assert a wrong tier or a non-verbatim quote. Measured on the first clean run: **all sections present, 0 tier-violations, 0 non-verbatim quotes, 0 invalid IDs, 158 citations verified — and the fastest path yet (~260s).**

**Lesson.** *The durable moat isn't "the AI writes guides." It's "the AI writes guides whose every claim is provably traced to the right source," delivered by a system where hallucinated citations cannot be represented.* This mirrors Anthropic's production "CitationAgent" pattern — separate the composing from the attributing — but implemented deterministically, with no extra LLM call.

---

## Decision Point 7 — When two evaluators disagree

**Situation.** The deterministic validator reported zero citation violations. The LLM-based evaluator reported failures. They disagreed — twice. Investigation showed the LLM judge produced a **false negative** (it had been fed a truncated half of a 47K-char document and called the missing half "absent sections") and, separately, a **false positive** (it flagged a quote as fabricated that was, verbatim, in the ticket's AC).

**The decision.** The **deterministic gate is the publish authority; the LLM evaluator is advisory only.** When they disagree, the deterministic check wins; the disagreement is logged for calibration, never auto-flips a verdict.

**Lesson.** *A model-based grader is a hypothesis, not a verdict.* For provenance — an exact, checkable property — a string match against ground truth beats an LLM, and is cheaper and reproducible.

---

## Decision Point 8 — When should the agent call live Jira?

**Situation.** With the demo running on cached Jira, the question arose: should the agent be "smart enough" to decide when to make a live MCP call for fresh data?

**The reframe.** This is a **cache-coherence problem, not an AI-cleverness problem** — the same problem CDNs solved with TTLs, cache-miss-on-demand, and stale-while-revalidate. Making "call live Jira" a free-form LLM judgment would re-introduce exactly the non-determinism, latency, and failure modes the team had engineered out.

**The decision.** *The model classifies its own grounding confidence; the harness owns the action.* The model raises a flag (it already does, via `[CITE:id]` vs `[TO VERIFY]`); a deterministic policy decides whether that flag trips a live call. For the **demo, live calls are off** (a frozen export needs no freshness, and a live call could fail on stage). Live escalation is a **production** capability, gated on one decisive fact: because guides are **SME-reviewed before shipping**, the human *is* the freshness check — so live MCP is a nicety, not a requirement.

**Lesson.** *Reserve model judgment for the genuinely open-ended part; make the deterministic part deterministic.*

---

## Decision Point 9 — The silent-failure landmine (generalization)

**Situation.** The demo cached only one module (Item Management). A pre-Thursday assessment surfaced the highest-stakes risk: if someone uploaded a **different-topic** transcript, the tools would still match against the *wrong module's* cache — and because the grounding gate only checks quote-and-tier integrity, **it would show all-green over content cited to the wrong feature.** A verified, confident, wrong guide.

**The decision.** Two fixes, both enabled by an asset the team already had — a 3,661-row "all-fields" Jira CSV export spanning every module and ticket type:
1. **Build offline fixtures for all seven roadmap modules directly from the CSV** (no live Jira needed), honoring its many gotchas (notably: *Acceptance Criteria is the second of two AC columns — the first is empty*).
2. **Add real module filtering** so the tools can never surface another module's tickets.

**Lesson.** *The dangerous failure is the silent one.* An honest crash beats a confident lie; the fix turned a silent wrong-module hallucination into a safe, correct, multi-module capability.

---

## Decision Point 10 — Trust as a product surface

**Situation.** With grounding solved, the question became how to *expose* trust to non-technical stakeholders, and how to ship guides responsibly.

**The decisions.**
- **Release model:** a guide can be released for use and download, but stamped **"Pending Review by SME"** — the machine grounding gate is the floor to release; human sign-off is the floor that's still pending. The PDF carries the banner; the Library shows the badge.
- **Evals as a first-class surface:** a layman-readable **Grounding Trust Score** (a grade, three plain-English cards, a clean table) — engineering depth available but hidden unless something fails.
- **Eval suite as infrastructure:** a deterministic regression suite (15 checks, zero LLM, runs in seconds) that pins every historical failure and gates CI; plus a capability harness logging cost and pass/pass-consistency per run.

**Lesson.** *Evals aren't QA polish — they're the trust guarantee, and they belong in the product, not just the pipeline.*

---

## Decision Point 11 — The review gate: approve, edit, and a router that can't weaken grounding

**Situation.** The machine grounding gate proved a guide was *provably grounded*, but the demo still *auto-released* every passing draft into the Library stamped "Pending Review by SME" — a label, not a step. The team wanted the human sign-off to be real: a reviewer should read the guide and either **approve** it or **offer edits** *before* it reaches the Library. Two design forks opened immediately: how should "offer edits" work without re-opening the door to hallucination, and should a new agent decide whether an edit needs re-checking?

**The temptation.** Ship a free-form rich-text editor ("let them just fix it") and add a full reviewing *agent* that re-reads the whole guide after every edit to decide if it's still fine.

**The decision.** Three moves, each chosen to keep correctness a property of the *system*, not the *model*:

1. **Approve is the only gate into the Library.** The deterministic grounding gate is the *machine floor* (a draft can't be reviewed until every citation is verbatim and correctly-tiered); a human approval is the *floor into the Library*. Unapproved drafts never appear there — they sit in an "Awaiting review" queue. This is the textbook **"validating" approval gate** for medium-stakes, reversible content: single approver, others get visibility not approval rights, so the gate never silently becomes a queue.

2. **Edits are AI-assisted, and structurally can't break grounding.** "Offer edits" is a plain-English instruction, not a text box. An **edit agent** emits targeted find/replace ops and is *barred from touching the `<!-- Source -->` citations or inventing uncited facts* (it refuses, or inserts a `[TO VERIFY]` marker — never a fabricated citation). Critically, the **deterministic grounding gate re-runs after every edit** (it's free), so an edit that breaks a citation is caught and blocks approval. The verbatim quotes live *inside* the citation comments the agent can't touch — so a reworded paragraph is safe *by construction*, exactly as generation is.

3. **The edit-triage "agent" is a classifier, not an agent — and advisory only.** A lightweight router tags each edit `stylistic` (wording/format — fast-path to approve) or `substantive` (a claim, number, label, or step may have changed — read it closely), defaulting to `substantive` when unsure. This is Anthropic's **"routing" workflow** — "find the simplest solution possible, and only increase complexity when needed," because "agentic systems often trade latency and cost for better task performance." A full agent here would re-introduce the non-determinism and latency the project spent ten decision points removing. The router can only send an edit for an *extra* check; it can **never** skip the grounding gate.

**The reframe that settled it.** The worry was "won't re-checking every stylistic tweak slow the product?" But the grounding gate is *deterministic and free*, so it always runs; the classifier doesn't gate it — it only decides whether a human should look harder. Speed is gained on safe edits without ever letting the router weaken the guarantee. And because the whole gate sits *downstream* of the generator, it doesn't touch what the regression suite measures.

**The one honest gap.** The router is advisory, but it can still mislabel. The dangerous direction is **under-triggering** — calling a substantive edit "stylistic." The fix (per "Demystifying evals") is the router's *own* balanced eval (~20–50 real stylistic-vs-substantive edits, with an always-"substantive" baseline that must fail the stylistic half), tracking under-trigger rate as the gated metric. That eval is specced, not yet built — logged as the next step, not hidden.

**Lesson.** *A human gate is a product surface, not a label — but the way to add one without slowing the system (or moving your eval scores) is to keep the new judgment advisory and deterministic where it matters.* Approve is a real blocking step; edits ride the same ground-by-construction rails as generation; the router is the simplest classifier that works. The moat held: even a human-requested AI edit cannot represent a mis-citation.

## Key lessons

1. **"Reads well" is not the standard; "matches what it cites" is.** Customer-facing tone creates pressure to smooth over evidence gaps — design against it.
2. **Ground by construction, not by instruction.** If the model *can* express a hallucinated citation, eventually it will. Remove the channel.
3. **Comprehensive is often a bug.** Bounding scope improved speed *and* quality.
4. **Measure the real bottleneck.** "Slow" was invisible reasoning, not visible output.
5. **A model-based grader is a hypothesis.** For exact properties, deterministic checks are the authority; LLM judges are advisory and must be calibrated.
6. **The silent failure is the dangerous one.** Make the system crash honestly rather than pass confidently on wrong data.
7. **Decide the workflow fact first.** "SME-reviewed vs auto-published" determined whether live freshness was required at all.
8. **Reuse the assets you already have.** The CSV and the second-brain wiki were the same lineage as the raw feed — leverage beat rebuild.
9. **A human gate is a step, not a label — and the new judgment should stay advisory.** Make approval actually block entry to the Library; route edits with the simplest classifier that works (not another agent); and keep the deterministic gate, not the router, as the thing that protects grounding. Add agency only where the task is genuinely open-ended.

## Discussion questions

1. The team made mis-citation *impossible by construction* rather than training/prompting the model to cite well. Where else in your products should correctness be a property of the *system* rather than the *model* — and what does that cost in flexibility?
2. The deterministic gate guarantees *provenance* (right quote, right tier) but not *relevance* (the right quote attached to the right claim) or *coverage* (nothing important omitted). How should a team decide which guarantees to automate and which to leave to human review?
3. Live data is fresher but riskier; cached data is reliable but can go stale. Given an SME reviews every output, was deferring live Jira the right call — and how would the answer change if the guides were auto-published?
4. The project repeatedly slowed to "do more research" on decisions the team had effectively already made. When does gathering more evidence become a way of avoiding a decision?
5. Trust was eventually made a visible product surface (the Trust Score, the "Pending SME Review" stamp). Does surfacing uncertainty build credibility with customers, or erode it?
6. The edit-triage router is *advisory* — it can route an edit for an extra check but can never skip the grounding gate. When is "advisory, with a deterministic backstop" the right ceiling for a model's authority, and when does it just add a step users learn to ignore?
