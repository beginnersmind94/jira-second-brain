The shape of eligibility in this product

Eligibility isn't a pipeline; it's a small system. There are three sources that can populate a student's eligibility record, one record that lives in the database, and four downstream consumers that read from it. One of those consumers — Verification — can also write back to revise the record. That's the only loop.

The dashed arrow is the only feedback loop: Verification can revise a record after sampling it. Everything else is clean source → record → consumer. Once that shape is in your head, every later detail has a slot it fits into.

Module 1

The eligibility record

By the end: You can name the three eligibility statuses, the three paths a student takes to one of them, and explain why "is this going to mess up my claim?" is the question behind almost every customer call.

What the product stores

For every enrolled student, the product stores a current eligibility record with four fields that matter:

  • Status — Free, Reduced, or Paid. This drives POS pricing and claim math.
  • Basis — why the kid landed there. Income calculation? SNAP match? Foster designation? Carryover from last year? The Basis is more useful for triage than the Status, because it tells you which path the record came from.
  • Start date / end date — eligibility windows. A kid can be Free in September and Paid in March if their application expired and a new one didn't come in.
  • Source on each change — Application #123, Direct Cert File #444, Manual Change, or Carryover. Every status change writes a history row.

The three possible Statuses, and what each one drives:

Why this matters

When a customer calls panicked about an eligibility bug, the question behind the question is almost always: "Is this going to mess up my claim?" Districts file monthly claims for federal reimbursement based on meals served by status. A wrong Status on one student is nothing. A systematic error across a site for a month — a DC file that didn't match, a CEP percentage off by a percentage point — translates to thousands of dollars the district can't easily recover. Three paths to a Status

A student's Status can only come from one of three places — the three sources in the system map (Orientation):

Direct Cert

State sends a roster of SNAP / TANF / Medicaid / foster kids. Matched students get Free, automatically, no application required. Module 3.

→
Application

Household submits a Free/Reduced application. System runs an income or categorical test and assigns a Status. Module 4.

→
CEP / blanket

Some sites operate under the Community Eligibility Provision — every kid eats free, no per-kid eligibility test. Module 5.

These don't compete in a fixed precedence. Direct Cert usually outranks an income-based application (because DC is automatic free). CEP overrides everything at participating sites — there is no per-kid Status check at a CEP site, just a site-wide claiming percentage. But effective dates matter: an application processed today doesn't retroactively change a meal served yesterday.

Federal context — why USDA pays for any of this in the first place

Legal basis: the Richard B. Russell National School Lunch Act, modernized by the Healthy, Hunger-Free Kids Act of 2010. Implementation rules live in 7 CFR Parts 210 (NSLP) and 245 (eligibility). USDA's Food and Nutrition Service (FNS) publishes the operational guidance. You won't cite these in code review; you'll see them invoked when a customer or compliance person asks "is the product allowed to do X?" The answer almost always comes from Part 245.

Implementation intention

When a teammate asks "why is this student showing as Paid?" — before you guess, you will check three things in order: (1) is there an active Direct Cert match? (2) is there an approved application for this academic year? (3) is the student's site under CEP / P2? Status flows from the answer to those three, in that order of precedence.

Checkpoint Try it
A customer says a kid is showing as Paid but "we know they're on SNAP." Where do you look first?

Right. Basis tells you which of the three paths (DC / application / CEP) produced the record. Status tells you what they pay; Basis tells you which trail to follow.

Module 2

Categorical vs. income-based

By the end: You can read an eligibility record's Basis field and explain in one sentence why the system made the determination it did.

Categorical = automatically free, no income test

Some households are categorically eligible for free school meals because they already qualify for another federal program with a stricter or equivalent income test. USDA's reasoning: if SNAP already verified their income, school meals shouldn't make them prove it again. The federal categorical sources are:

FDPIRFood Distribution Program on Indian ReservationsState or tribal DC file; application with case #

In the product, the Basis field surfaces the source. You'll see Free (DC SNAP) or Free (DC MEDICAID) for direct-certified students, and Categorical: SNAP or Categorical: Homeless for application-routed categorical determinations.

Why these sources, and not just income everywhere?

The categorical list exists because USDA decided that if another federal program already verified a household's income (SNAP, TANF, FDPIR), school meals shouldn't make them prove it again. SNAP direct certification is mandatory for states — Healthy, Hunger-Free Kids Act 2010 made that the law. TANF and FDPIR DC are permitted but not required, so most but not all states do them. Medicaid DC is a demonstration project — only 44 states participate, and product behavior around Medicaid lookups is configurable per state. Don't assume Medicaid DC is on; check the district's configuration. Foster / homeless / migrant / runaway / Head Start come in via written designation from the responsible agency, not via a state file.

Income-based: applications and the IEG

If a kid isn't categorically eligible, the household can apply. The application asks for everyone in the household and their gross income (with frequency: weekly, every two weeks, twice a month, monthly, annual). The system compares the converted annual income against the federal Income Eligibility Guidelines (IEG) for that household size.

The IEG is a two-line scale, both expressed as multiples of the federal poverty level (FPL):

Mixed applications and the "free for one, reduced for the other" edge case

An application can list multiple kids in the same household, and one of them can be categorical (foster, say) while the others aren't. Federal rule: SNAP / TANF / FDPIR extend categorically to the whole household. Any other categorical source — foster, homeless, migrant, runaway, Head Start — is per-student only. So if an application has a foster kid plus a non-foster sibling with income data, the foster kid is Free (categorical) and the sibling's status comes from the income test on the rest of the household. The product handles this by re-running the determination per student after categorical flags resolve. When a customer questions a determination across siblings on the same app, that's where to look first.

Reading a Basis field in practice

When you open a student's eligibility detail, read the Basis field before the Status. Status alone tells you what the kid pays. Basis tells you why, and why changes the answer to every follow-up question — including "is verification going to touch this app?" (Categorical apps are mostly exempt; income apps are the verification pool, Module 6.)

Checkpoint Try it
A student's record shows Free (DC SNAP). Will this student show up in November's verification sample?

Right. Verification is an income-application audit. Anything that came via Direct Cert (or via foster/homeless/migrant/runaway designation) is exempt — the state has already verified them through SNAP/TANF/FDPIR, so there's nothing to re-prove.

Module 3

Direct Certification

By the end: You can describe the DC file → match → eligibility pipeline, name three reasons a real SNAP kid might come back Unmatched, and know what the product does about it.

The pipeline

State agencies (usually Department of Human Services or its equivalent) maintain rosters of households participating in SNAP, TANF, FDPIR, foster care, and — in 44 states — Medicaid. Each state sets its own DC file format, cadence, and access method. The district pulls or receives the file, drops it into the product, and:

1 · Ingest

File is parsed into a structured DC import record. Each row is a candidate match.

→
2 · Match

System fuzzy-matches against enrolled student roster on name, DOB, address. Produces Matched, Unmatched, or Multiple Matches.

→
3 · Apply

Matched students get a Free eligibility record with Basis = DC [Source]. Notifications fire to households via Family Hub if configured.

Cybersoft Cybersoft sets up the import format per state during onboarding. A Central Office Admin District uploads the file on whatever cadence the state issues it — monthly is typical, weekly is common for SNAP. The matching algorithm runs automatically; the user sees a Direct Certification page with a grid of all DC imports for the year.

Federal rule for SNAP DC matching frequency: an LEA must conduct SNAP matching at least three times per school year — at the beginning of the year, three months after the initial effort, and six months after the initial effort. Once DC documentation is received for a student, the LEA must apply free benefits as soon as possible and no later than three operating days — note this is shorter than the 10-day rule for income applications.

The product's Notifications page District aggregates approval letters across Applications, Direct Certification, Carryover, and Outreach — all in one place, organized by tab. DC-specific notifications can be sent on demand or set to fire automatically when the auto-notification setting is on. If the household has a linked Family Hub parent account, the notice also lands in Family Hub. Parent

The federal benchmark

Since SY 2013-2014, USDA requires states to direct-certify 95% of school-age children in SNAP households. States below the threshold submit a Continuous Improvement Plan to FNS each year. The product doesn't enforce this — that's a state-level reporting metric — but you'll see it referenced in customer conversations and in any conversation about state reporting features.

Why matching is fuzzy on purpose

The single most-asked DC question — "why isn't this kid Free yet?" — almost always comes back to a matching failure, not a bug. The product fuzzy-matches on name + DOB + address because state SNAP rolls and district SIS records drift constantly: different middle names, address moves, typos on either side, junior/senior suffixes, hyphens, nicknames. An exact-match algorithm would silently drop real eligible kids and the district would carry the cost. The fuzzy threshold (and the 2-of-3 sibling-extension rule below) are tradeoffs between false positives (extending Free to siblings who shouldn't get it) and false negatives (missing siblings who should). When a ticket says "this kid should be Free but isn't," the question underneath is almost always which side of one of those tradeoffs the algorithm landed on for the specific case. The product gives you the data to answer that: open the DC import, look at the row count for matched, unmatched, and multiple.

The common ways a real SNAP kid lands Unmatched:

Name mismatch

Roster: "Jose Garcia Jr." Student record: "Joe Garcia." Different enough that fuzzy match drops below threshold.

DOB typo

One-digit typo on either side. Fuzzy match scoring weighs DOB heavily, so the row falls out.

SIS roster lag

Kid enrolled last week. SIS sync runs nightly. DC file ran Tuesday. No enrolled student to match against. Re-run after the next sync.

Multiple potential matches

Twins, same DOB, similar names. Flagged for human review on Pending Students. Don't auto-resolve.

Inactive accounts

Student withdrew, account wasn't deactivated. DC matches the old record. The match view shows Active/Inactive so the user picks the right one.

Stale DC over fresh application

A new application approves Reduced; an older DC match still says Free. Source precedence + effective dates decide which wins — usually DC, but dates matter.

Household extension — only SNAP, FDPIR, TANF

When a DC match lands on one kid, the system System can auto-extend free eligibility to siblings — but only for SNAP, FDPIR, and TANF, because federal law specifically makes those categorical at the household level. Foster, homeless, migrant, runaway, and Head Start status do not extend to siblings. Customers occasionally expect a foster-kid match to flip siblings to Free; it won't, and that's the correct legal behavior — don't "fix" it.

For the sources that do extend, the in-product trigger is the DCSBAUTO system setting plus a match on at least 2 of {same household, same guardian, same address}. The job runs daily after each DC import. Each extension writes a history row with the matching criteria so an auditor can reconstruct the decision. The 2-of-3 rule is the same false-positive / false-negative tradeoff as the matching threshold above: looser criteria catch more real siblings and also catch more wrong ones.

Secondary matching and Pending Students

If the initial pass leaves candidates that didn't score high enough for auto-apply but might still be the right student, a secondary matching pass System retries with looser criteria for human review. Users can also trigger a manual search from the Pending Students view — that's the screen support steers customers to when "we sent a DC file, why isn't this kid Free?" comes in. Don't ask them to re-upload; ask them to look at the DC import row counts and the Pending Students list.

Triaging a DC complaint

When a customer reports "the state says this kid is on SNAP but the system says Paid": (1) open the DC page, confirm the most recent file was ingested; (2) search Unmatched and Multiple Matches for the student's name; (3) check the student's enrollment date against the file date. If all three look fine, then it's a bug. Most of the time one of those three is the answer.

Checkpoint Try it
A state SNAP file just landed. The customer is sure Mary Garcia is on SNAP — but she shows as Unmatched. What's the most likely single cause?

Right. DC matches fail far more often from data drift than from product bugs. Open the DC import, look at Unmatched, check her name and DOB against the state file. The "no household application" answer is a red herring — DC is supposed to replace the application requirement.

The three buckets every stuck application falls into

Applications break in three predictable ways:

  1. The matched student isn't actually this kid. Twins, transfers, name changes. Tooled in the product via the Pending Students manual-match flow.
  2. The household lied or made an honest mistake on income. Not visible at application time; surfaces months later in Verification (Module 6).
  3. A categorical claim couldn't be confirmed. "We get SNAP" with no case number, no DC match. Stuck on a Pending Reason until something resolves.

When a processor escalates a stuck application, the first question is always "which of those three buckets?" The product is doing what the workflow requires; the "fix" is usually a missing data input, not a code change.

Triaging a stuck application

Open the application detail, scan Pending Reasons, identify which one is blocking. Don't assume system bug until every Pending Reason has been addressed.

Checkpoint Try it
An application has been sitting in "Submitted" for a week and won't advance to Processed. Where do you look first?

Right. Applications stall on Pending Reasons by design — missing signature, household-size discrepancy, unconfirmed categorical claim, etc. The system is enforcing the federal requirement that benefits be applied within 10 operating days of a complete application; the "complete" qualifier matters.

Module 5

Community Eligibility Provision (CEP)

By the end: You can do the ISP math by hand, explain when CEP makes financial sense for a district, and know the four-year cycle gotchas.

What CEP is

Community Eligibility Provision is a federal option that lets a school, group of schools, or whole district serve every student free meals — no applications, no eligibility tests per kid — and claim federal reimbursement at a percentage. It was created by the Healthy, Hunger-Free Kids Act of 2010 and expanded by the 2023 final rule. The trade-off: the district stops collecting per-student eligibility data and accepts a claim percentage based on its Identified Student Percentage (ISP), locked in for up to four years.

The ISP math

The ISP is calculated as of April 1 of the school year before CEP starts:

ISP formula ISP = (Identified Students on April 1 ÷ Enrolled Students on April 1) × 100

Identified Students means kids who would be free without any household application — directly certified via SNAP, TANF, FDPIR, foster, homeless, migrant, runaway, Head Start. Income-based free determinations don't count toward the ISP. That's the whole point: CEP is for sites where so many kids are categorically poor that running individual applications is wasted overhead.

To claim, multiply the ISP by the CEP multiplier:

Federal claiming percentage Free claiming % = ISP × 1.6 (capped at 100%)
Paid claiming % = 100% − Free %

USDA can set the multiplier anywhere between 1.3 and 1.6; the current multiplier is 1.6 and has been stable. Once a site elects CEP, the 1.6 multiplier is locked for the entire four-year cycle.

Worked example A site has 1,000 enrolled students on April 1. Of those, 380 are identified (DC-matched).
ISP = 380 ÷ 1,000 = 38.0%
Free claiming % = 38.0% × 1.6 = 60.8%
Paid claiming % = 100% − 60.8% = 39.2%
For every 100 reimbursable meals served at this site, the district claims 60.8 at the federal Free rate and 39.2 at the federal Paid rate — regardless of which specific kids ate.

The 25% threshold (and why it matters)

Pre-2023, a site needed ISP ≥ 40% to qualify for CEP. In September 2023, USDA's final rule lowered the threshold to 25%, effective October 26, 2023. This roughly doubled the universe of sites that could elect CEP. The product was updated to reflect the new threshold. A site between 15% and 25% ISP is "nearly eligible" and tracked separately by USDA.

Election deadline and cycle

A district intending to elect CEP for the next school year must notify the state agency by June 30 of the prior school year, with documentation of identified and enrolled counts as of April 1. Once elected, the site runs CEP for a four-year cycle. During the cycle, the district recalculates ISP every April 1; if it goes up, the district can adopt the higher figure for the rest of the cycle. If it goes down, the original (locked) ISP still applies.

Year four: the grace year

At the end of the four-year cycle, the site redetermines using current ISP. If it has dropped below 25% but is still at least 15% as of April 1 of year four, the site can elect one additional grace year of CEP. If during the grace year the site does not regain 25% ISP, it returns to household applications the following school year. The grace year is a one-time bridge, not a renewable status — customers in year four who are flirting with the 25% threshold need to know this is the last out before re-collecting applications.

Annual public-notification dates

CEP has its own rhythm of administrative dates worth knowing because they show up in customer questions and in any reporting feature touching this area:

  • April 1: identified-student / enrolled-student count snapshot for ISP calculation.
  • April 15: LEAs submit lists of schools by ISP band (≥25%, 15-25%, year-4 grace) to the state.
  • May 1: state agencies publish the required lists publicly.
  • June 30: LEAs intending to elect CEP for next school year submit letter of intent.

Why CEP is a four-year commitment — and why "unlock CEP mid-cycle" isn't a small ask

"Should we go CEP?" is a financial decision, not a software decision. The district's CFO models lost paid-meal revenue plus the cost of feeding higher-income kids for free against the saved overhead (no applications, no verification, simpler operations) plus the federal claim revenue from CEP percentages. They build a multi-year model, and CEP is a multi-year financial bet — that's why USDA locks the ISP, the multiplier, and the participation status for four years at a time. The product treats those percentages as commitments, not settings, which is why "let us unlock CEP mid-cycle" feature requests don't get done lightly. The grace-year mechanism exists precisely because the cycle is rigid: it's USDA's one regulated escape hatch when ISP slips at year-end.

Don't advise customers on whether CEP is right for them — that's their CFO's job, with FRAC or USDA modeling tools. Stay in your lane: given the district has chosen CEP for site X, does the product reflect that correctly?

How the product reflects all this

A Central Office Admin District designates CEP/P2 status on the Special Assistance Programs configuration page, per site, with start and end dates and monthly claiming percentages. Sites not selected default to zero CEP %; selecting "All" turns it on for everyone. When a Free or Reduced percentage changes, Paid recalculates automatically. Downstream, the claim generation script reads those per-site percentages and applies them to meal counts when building the claim — sites not on CEP/P2 are unaffected. Year-begin carryover also has a CEP-specific branch: a system setting decides whether students at CEP sites get an auto-applied Free + Carryover record on July 1, separate from the usual Free/Reduced carryover (Module 7).

Triaging a CEP claim discrepancy

When a CEP customer asks "why don't my claim totals match my POS totals?", open Special Assistance Programs configuration for the affected site, read off the Free/Reduced/Paid percentages, and confirm the math: meal counts × site percentages = claimed amounts. The discrepancy is almost always in the percentages or the date range, not the meal counts themselves.

Checkpoint Try it
A site has an Identified Student Percentage of 30% as of April 1. Under CEP, what's their free claiming percentage for the year?

Right. 30 × 1.6 = 48% free claiming, 52% paid claiming. Every kid eats free at the cafeteria; the federal claim splits the meal counts according to those site-wide percentages. The 1.6 multiplier is what makes CEP financially viable for districts with moderate identified-student counts.

Module 6

Verification

By the end: You can describe the federal sample-size rule, name the three sampling methods, and explain why November 15 is the deadline everyone fears.

What it is and why

Verification is the annual, federally-mandated process of asking a sample of approved income-based applications to prove what they reported. It exists because USDA can't afford to fully audit every household, so it audits a statistical sample — and the rules force districts to weight the sample toward applications most likely to contain errors. Verification does not apply to households where all kids are determined eligible based on SNAP/FDPIR/TANF documentation, foster, homeless, migrant, or runaway status — those are exempt. Verification also does not delay approval: an application is approved on its own timeline (the 10-day rule from Module 4), and verification happens later, against the already-approved pool.

Sample size

The default rule (called Error-Prone Sampling) is:

Sample size · standard method The lesser of 3% of all approved applications on file as of October 1, selected from error-prone applications, or 3,000 error-prone applications, whichever is smaller. If error-prone applications are insufficient, fill the sample randomly from other approved applications.

An application is error-prone if reported monthly income is within $100 of the applicable IEG threshold (free or reduced), or annual income within $1,200. These are the applications most likely to flip eligibility based on a small data correction, which is why USDA forces districts to audit them first.

Two alternates

Districts meeting certain qualifying criteria can use one of two alternate sample sizes instead:

MethodSample sizePulled from
Error-Prone (Standard)Lesser of 3% (of all approved apps) or 3,000 error-prone appsError-prone first; random fill if short
Alternate One — RandomLesser of 3% or 3,000, all selected at randomRandom from all approved apps
Alternate Two — FocusedLesser of 1% or 1,000 of error-prone apps, plus lesser of 0.5% or 500 of case-number applications (SNAP/FDPIR/TANF case number on file)Error-prone + case-number pools

Note the precision on Alternate Two: the second pool is case-number applications — applications where the household provided a SNAP/FDPIR/TANF case number on the form. It is not the same as all categorically-routed kids. Directly certified students never enter the verification pool at all. The product's Verification module supports each method via the Sampling Wizard. State rounding rules vary — Texas, for instance, will issue an audit finding if Alternate Two rounding is performed twice (once on each pool) rather than once on the sum. The product exposes this as a system setting, Verification Rounding for Alternate Two, with options Once / Twice and a default of Once.

How the product runs it

A district admin District sets up verification once per school year — sample process, schedule, rolling-vs-final, sample size system settings. The product:

  • Auto-generates samples via a scheduled background job at midnight on the configured date. System
  • Tracks responses on the Verification Tracking page: response received, income confirmed, income corrected, no response.
  • Caps deletions: a processor can delete up to 5% of sampled applications from the sample (e.g., student withdrew). Above 5%, the delete button disables. Controlled by the MAXRMVSAMP system setting.
  • Counts down on the dashboard: a "Days until Verification Begins" CTA shows until the final sample date; a "Days until Verification Ends" CTA shows after, until 14 days past the response end date.

Rolling verification

Some districts run rolling verification: instead of one big sample on October 31 or so, they pull samples on a schedule (8/1, 9/1, final 10/1) and process them progressively. The product's sampling logic handles the date-range arithmetic — each sample pulls only from apps approved between the prior sample's end date and the new sample date — to avoid over-sampling the same applications.

Why a state audit finding is the thing to avoid

A state audit finding on verification doesn't shut down the program, but it costs money. The state can require the district to repay federal reimbursement for any meal served after November 15 if verification wasn't complete. For a mid-sized district that's tens of thousands of dollars, sometimes more. That's why every Q3-Q4 verification ticket gets escalated, why customer-facing prose around verification is conservative ("must," not "should"), and why the 5% deletion cap is enforced at the product level — districts under deadline pressure will try to "lose" inconvenient applications from the sample, and federal rules don't permit that. The product enforces the cap so districts don't accidentally cross it; the cap is a feature, not a constraint to remove.

Triaging a verification ticket between August and November

Treat it as time-critical, not feature-request-critical. Identify whether the customer is on Error-Prone (Standard), Alternate One, or Alternate Two before suggesting any workflow change — the three methods have meaningfully different sample math, and the wrong answer compounds into an audit finding.

Checkpoint Try it
It's November 20. A district hasn't completed verification and didn't request a state extension. What happens?

Right. The Nov 15 deadline has financial teeth. For a mid-sized district that's tens of thousands of dollars of reimbursement at risk per month overdue. It's also why the 5% sample-deletion cap in the product is enforced — districts under deadline pressure will otherwise try to "lose" inconvenient applications.

POS and Meal Exceptions

A POS transaction prices a meal based on the student's eligibility at the time of the swipe. If a household's eligibility changes mid-day (a DC file came in, an application was processed), the cashier might have already rung up transactions at the old price. The product tracks these as Meal Exceptions: meals served at a different eligibility than the student's current status. Users can view them, select which ones to re-price to current status, and bulk-update — preserving an audit trail. Don't bulk-update without checking dates; sometimes the "wrong" eligibility was correct at transaction time.

Year-begin carryover (the July 1 ritual)

Federal rules require districts to give households a grace period at the start of each school year so they have time to reapply. On July 1, the system System automatically:

  • Inserts new eligibility records for every student who was Free or Reduced on June 30, with the same status, start date 7/1, end date per the district's carryover end date, Reason = Carryover.
  • If the "CEP Sites - Automatically apply Free Grace Period (Carryover) eligibility during Year Begin" system setting is on, also inserts a Free + Carryover record for every student at a CEP site.
  • Inserts a Paid - Default record for every other student.

The carryover end date is district-configurable (often 30 calendar days into the new year, but states vary). When a household reapplies and the new app is processed, the new determination replaces the carryover record. When the carryover expires without a new application, status flips to Paid - Default.

The Eligibility Changes widget

The Insights dashboard's Eligibility Changes widget District logs every recent change with: student name, ID, site, grade, former status, new status, reason, change date, and source (Application #123, DC File #444, Manual Change). Default view is the last 7 days; the date range is configurable. This is the fastest way to answer "did this student's status actually change, and when, and from what?"

The Roster report (point-in-time)

The Eligibility Roster lets a user pull a full list of students with their eligibility status and reason as of a specific date — current or historical. Filters by site, grade, account status, eligibility, basis. This is the report state auditors request, and it's the source of truth for "what was true on date X."

The Notifications hub

One page, four tabs: Applications, Direct Certification, Carryover, Outreach. Each tab manages notifications for its scope. Outreach is the catch-all for marketing letters and "you haven't applied yet" nudges to households still on Paid status.

The Edit Check Worksheet (Accountability cross-reference)

Daily meal counts by Free / Reduced / Paid get compared against site eligible-student counts and attendance factors on the Edit Check Worksheet. If counts spike above what eligibility alone would support, the worksheet flags it — that's a state audit signal. Eligibility data flowing wrong into here is one of the most expensive bugs to ship; double-check anytime you touch the eligibility-to-claims handoff.

Why "I tested it in the Eligibility module" is not the success condition

It's tempting to treat eligibility as a self-contained module — read in, written back, done. It isn't. Every change ripples to five places. When you ship a change to how eligibility is recorded — a new basis value, a different effective-date rule, a new categorical source, a tweak to Carryover — the test plan needs to include all of these: did POS price correctly at the next transaction? did the next claim build include the change? did the Roster reflect it as of the right date? did the Family Hub notification go out? did the state export pick up the new value? Skipping any one of those is how regressions ship. The Eligibility Changes widget is your friend here — it surfaces the write; everything after is a downstream consumer that has to be checked separately.

Shipping an eligibility change safely

When you change anything that writes an eligibility record, trace the change through all five downstream consumers (POS pricing for new transactions, Eligibility Changes widget, Roster report, next claim build, Family Hub notification) before marking the work done.

Checkpoint Try it
You shipped a fix to year-begin Carryover. Which of these is not sufficient as a test before marking the work done?

Right — C alone is not enough. Eligibility writes ripple into POS, Claims, Reports, Family Hub, and state exports. Visual confirmation in the Eligibility module proves the write happened; it doesn't prove the downstream consumers picked it up. A and B are real, useful checks; C is a necessary-but-insufficient one.
